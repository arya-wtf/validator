## Ideas Input

AI Client Project Copilot

Sebuah mini web app untuk solo designer atau small studio yang membantu mereka mengatur scope, komunikasi client, feedback, timeline risk, dan handoff dari awal sampai akhir project.

Bukan app untuk “manage task internal”, tapi app untuk mengelola hubungan dan ekspektasi client supaya project tidak chaos.

Simple explanation

Client Chaos Manager adalah AI-powered client management tool untuk freelance designer dan small studio. User memasukkan detail project, scope, timeline, deliverables, dan kondisi client. Lalu app akan membantu membuat kickoff document, revision rules, project update messages, feedback summaries, risk alerts, delay messages, dan final handoff checklist.

Jadi product app-nya bukan cuma template library, tapi workspace per project yang menghasilkan komunikasi dan dokumen penting sepanjang lifecycle project.

## Per-idea analysis

### Idea #1: AI Scoping Copilot

**Hypothesis:** If small agencies can turn messy project inquiries into budget-aware scope options and proposal-ready recommendations in under 20 minutes, some will pay immediately because this sits directly on the path to revenue.

**Assumed buyer:** agency founder, account lead, or marketing operator handling inbound leads without a dedicated solutions team

**Assumed pain:** A lead arrives with vague asks, limited budget, and unclear complexity; current workaround is asking a senior operator for help and rewriting proposals manually; cost is slower response time, weaker close rates, and accepting under-scoped work.

**Assumed pricing:** $79-$199/month per team, or $300-$800 one-time setup plus monthly subscription, because buyer already spends money on proposal work and can tie value to one saved or better-scoped deal.

**Distribution hypothesis:** First 10 buyers come from existing Elux network: Gunnar, TJ, Son, Jen, past repeat clients, plus founder-led outbound to small agencies on Upwork, Contra, and LinkedIn using live teardown demos of real inquiries.

**Strongest assumption:** Buyers will trust an AI-assisted scope recommendation enough to use it in client-facing proposal prep.

**Weakest assumption:** Solo designers are not the best first market; agencies with repeat scoping pain are much more likely to pay than freelancers who still improvise manually.

**Suggested Gate 3 test:** Manual Service - run 10 done-for-you scoping sessions from real inquiries, charge at least 5 buyers $100-$250 each or convert 3 into monthly pilot users within 21 days.

**Kill signals:**
- Prospects say they only need proposal writing, not scope analysis
- Users refuse to upload inquiry details because of client confidentiality
- Manual recommendations do not materially reduce back-and-forth with Arya or speed proposal turnaround by at least 50%

**Founder-fit notes:** This is closest to Dewi's stated pain and daily workflow. She already handles proposal prep, scope negotiation, and buyer communication. It also reduces dependence on Arya, which is a concrete internal ROI.

### Idea #2: Client Chaos Manager

**Hypothesis:** Freelance designers and small studios will pay for a project workspace that generates kickoff docs, revision rules, risk alerts, feedback summaries, and delay messages if it helps them keep clients aligned without extra admin.

**Assumed buyer:** freelance designer or 2-10 person design studio owner juggling multiple client projects

**Assumed pain:** Once a project starts, scope drift, unclear feedback, late assets, and awkward delay messages create chaos; current workaround is scattered Slack threads, Notion pages, and ad hoc templates; cost is client friction, revision creep, and margin loss.

**Assumed pricing:** $29-$99/month per studio based on active projects, because this is operational software with lower willingness to pay unless tied to fewer revision losses.

**Distribution hypothesis:** First 10 buyers come from design-heavy freelancers on Contra, Dribbble, and X, plus warm intros from existing clients who work with design teams.

**Strongest assumption:** Design freelancers feel enough ongoing pain from client communication to adopt a dedicated tool instead of patching together Notion, email, and Slack.

**Weakest assumption:** Dewi does not live in this workflow as the primary operator, so product intuition and distribution are secondhand rather than direct.

**Suggested Gate 3 test:** Pre-order - sell 5 paid pilot spots at $49-$99/month from a clickable demo plus 3 example outputs for kickoff, feedback summary, and delay management within 30 days.

**Kill signals:**
- Designers say Notion and Slack are good enough
- Prospects like the templates but do not want another workspace
- No one agrees to pay before integrations or automation exist

**Founder-fit notes:** The pain is real, but it is not Dewi's sharpest wedge. She avoids design execution and does not have a dense audience of freelance designers asking for this exact fix. Distribution is weaker than the agency-scoping angle.

### Idea #3: Project Proof Hub

**Hypothesis:** Small agencies will pay for a searchable internal-external project database that helps them retrieve relevant past work fast during sales conversations and proposal writing.

**Assumed buyer:** agency founder or bizdev/operator who needs to prove credibility quickly in sales calls and proposals

**Assumed pain:** Past work is spread across old docs, Slack, folders, and memory; current workaround is manually hunting examples and rewriting similar case-study blurbs; cost is slower proposal turnaround and weaker proof in buyer conversations.

**Assumed pricing:** $25-$79/month for a small team, or $250-$500 one-time setup service, because value is real but indirect unless paired with proposal generation.

**Distribution hypothesis:** First 10 buyers come from service businesses already juggling repeat project references: Elux peers, repeat clients with agency arms, and outbound to small studios that have 10+ past projects but weak case study systems.

**Strongest assumption:** Operators feel enough embarrassment and delay from scattered proof-of-work to pay for a dedicated repository instead of improving Google Drive and Notion.

**Weakest assumption:** As a standalone product, this may be seen as a nice-to-have admin layer rather than a budget-approved painkiller.

**Suggested Gate 3 test:** LOI - get 5 agencies to hand over past-project data for a paid setup or sign a letter of intent after seeing a mock search interface and one populated example workspace.

**Kill signals:**
- Prospects ask for this only as a feature inside proposal software
- Teams prefer manually curated portfolios over searchable internal references
- No buyer will pay for setup because organizing old work feels like housekeeping

**Founder-fit notes:** Dewi can execute this operationally and has direct access to agencies with this problem. But the standalone value is weaker than the scoping use case unless bundled into proposal workflows.

### Idea #4: Content Reuse OS

**Hypothesis:** Solo operators and lean agencies will pay for a system that turns one source asset into a repeatable multi-channel publishing workflow if it removes planning friction and keeps distribution consistent.

**Assumed buyer:** solo marketer, agency operator, or founder doing content without a full content team

**Assumed pain:** They publish a blog, note, or thread once and then fail to repurpose it across channels; current workaround is manual copy-paste and ad hoc planning; cost is inconsistent output and wasted content effort.

**Assumed pricing:** $19-$59/month, or a $150-$400 setup template package, because this competes with generic content tools and must stay lightweight.

**Distribution hypothesis:** First 10 buyers come from Dewi's own operator network, X content audience, and outbound to solo marketers already posting but inconsistently.

**Strongest assumption:** Operators care enough about workflow structure to pay for a dedicated reuse system instead of using docs, spreadsheets, and ChatGPT.

**Weakest assumption:** This market is crowded with low-cost alternatives, so differentiation is thin unless the workflow is tightly opinionated and niche-specific.

**Suggested Gate 3 test:** Pre-order - sell 10 setup packages at $150-$250 for a manual content reuse system before building software, then convert 3 to recurring software pilots.

**Kill signals:**
- Prospects want services done-for-you, not a system
- Buyers compare it to generic AI writing tools and push price below $20/month
- Manual setup does not improve publishing cadence after two weeks

**Founder-fit notes:** Execution fit is strong because Dewi already does text content end to end. But market noise is high and willingness to pay is usually lower than tools tied directly to closing client work.


## Ranking

**Idea #1 — PROCEED FIRST**
This is the closest match to Dewi's actual weekly pain, buyer access, and revenue path. It sits before project work starts, so buyers can justify spend against close rate, proposal speed, and scope control. You can test it manually with current contacts this week.

**Idea #3 — PROCEED IF X FAILS**
This has strong founder fit and easy execution, but weaker urgency as a standalone product. It becomes more attractive if buyers like the scoping workflow but mainly ask for searchable proof-of-work and reusable proposal references.

**Idea #4 — PARK**
Good operator fit, but crowded and price-sensitive. It is easier to sell as a service or template pack than software, and it does not map as directly to the budget-negotiation pain already showing up in client conversations.

**Idea #2 — KILL**
The idea is coherent, but the market access is weaker and the operator pain is less firsthand. Dewi's constraints also matter here: design is not her domain, and distribution to freelance designers will be slower than selling into agency scoping pain she already sees live.
