## Ideas Input

Prompt library and generator for landing pages, image assets, and video assets.

A curated prompt library with a built-in generator that helps freelance designers and small agencies create high-quality landing pages, campaign visuals, and short-form video assets faster.

Users can start from ready-to-use prompts or upload/use their existing assets and references to generate new variations, tweak sections, change visual styles, or repurpose content for different channels.

Target: freelance designers and small design agencies.
Pain to solve: creating good-quality landing pages and marketing assets takes too much time, especially when starting from scratch or manually rewriting prompts for every new project.

## Per-idea analysis

### Idea #1: Agency Prompt Ops Pack

**Hypothesis:** Small agencies will pay for a structured prompt system if it cuts revision cycles on landing page, ad creative, and short-form video variants for existing client work.

**Assumed buyer:** Founder-operator at a 2-10 person design or performance creative agency handling multiple client campaigns per month

**Assumed pain:** When a client asks for 5 more ad variants, a new LP direction, or channel-specific resizes, the team restarts from old chats, scattered docs, and references; workaround is copy-pasting prior prompts and manually briefing each variation; cost is 3-8 non-billable hours per project and slower turnaround.

**Assumed pricing:** $149-299 one-time template pack plus optional $79 onboarding call because agencies already buy process accelerators faster than they buy unproven software.

**Distribution hypothesis:** First 10 buyers come from current operator network: Gunnar, TJ, Jen, Son, and adjacent agency contacts who already ask for tool recommendations; close via Loom demos and direct Slack/DM outreach framed as an internal ops pack, not a product pitch.

**Strongest assumption:** Agencies care more about repeatable prompt workflows than about having the AI generation happen inside the product.

**Weakest assumption:** Design-led buyers will accept a text-heavy product from a non-designer if the operational value is clear.

**Suggested Gate 3 test:** Pre-order - sell 5 packs at $149+ from direct network within 14 days before building any generator.

**Kill signals:**
- Operators say their real bottleneck is design judgment, not prompt reuse
- Prospects ask to see generated visual quality rather than workflow structure
- Fewer than 3 of 20 warm contacts agree to a paid pilot or pre-order

**Founder-fit notes:** Fits Dewi's operator strength: packaging process, writing templates, and selling through direct relationships. Weak fit on visual credibility, so this must be positioned as production ops for teams already designing, not as a design inspiration product.

### Idea #2: Done-For-You Prompt System Setup

**Hypothesis:** The fastest path to evidence is a service where Dewi builds client-specific prompt libraries, asset naming rules, and reuse workflows for agencies already experimenting with AI creative tools.

**Assumed buyer:** Small agency founder or senior marketer with active client delivery and messy AI usage across team members

**Assumed pain:** The team has ChatGPT prompts, Midjourney snippets, brand references, and campaign examples spread across docs and personal accounts; workaround is each person inventing their own workflow; cost is inconsistent outputs, training overhead, and avoidable review rounds.

**Assumed pricing:** $300-800 setup fee per client plus optional $50-150 monthly maintenance because this sits inside current consulting behavior and can be sold as implementation, not software.

**Distribution hypothesis:** First 10 buyers come from existing clients and repeats who already ask for tools consultation; offer it as a scoped add-on during retainer reviews or project closeout, then productize the common setup components.

**Strongest assumption:** Current clients trust Dewi enough on tooling/process to buy a narrow AI workflow setup without needing deep technical or design authority.

**Weakest assumption:** The setup will deliver enough visible ROI that buyers see it as more than generic docs cleanup.

**Suggested Gate 3 test:** Manual Service - close 3 paid setups at $300+ within 10 days from existing network and capture before/after workflow data.

**Kill signals:**
- Warm clients like the idea but only want free recommendations over Slack
- Each setup becomes highly custom and cannot be templated after 3 deliveries
- No buyer can point to a concrete recurring asset workflow they want standardized

**Founder-fit notes:** Best fit with Dewi's current motion: tool consultation, process setup, client onboarding, and direct operator communication. It avoids heavy product build, avoids design execution, and turns existing trust into revenue fast.

### Idea #3: Prompt Library Membership for Freelancers

**Hypothesis:** Solo freelance designers will subscribe to a curated prompt library if it helps them ideate landing pages and campaign assets faster.

**Assumed buyer:** Solo freelance web or brand designer doing 1-4 client projects per month

**Assumed pain:** Freelancers feel slow when facing a blank page and manually rewriting prompts for every concept direction; workaround is browsing X threads, YouTube, and community prompt dumps; cost is fragmented inspiration and inconsistent speed gains.

**Assumed pricing:** $19-39 monthly membership because the buyer is budget-sensitive and expects low-cost educational products.

**Distribution hypothesis:** First 10 buyers come from content-led distribution on X/LinkedIn plus freelance communities, with a lead magnet of 10 free prompts.

**Strongest assumption:** Freelancers see enough recurring value in prompt curation to pay monthly instead of collecting free prompts.

**Weakest assumption:** Dewi has no strong existing distribution into freelance designer audiences, so customer acquisition would depend on content volume and design credibility.

**Suggested Gate 3 test:** Pre-order - get 25 email signups and 5 paid annual founding members at $99 within 21 days from content posts and direct outreach.

**Kill signals:**
- Posts get saves or likes but no paid conversions
- Prospects compare it to free Notion docs and Gumroad bundles
- Freelancers ask for design files/examples instead of prompt systems

**Founder-fit notes:** Weak fit because it leans on audience building and design-adjacent credibility rather than Dewi's current warm operator network. Low ACV also fights the $3k+ stability goal.

### Idea #4: Prompt Generator SaaS for LP/Image/Video

**Hypothesis:** A simple SaaS that turns briefs and existing assets into channel-specific prompts can win small agencies that are tired of scattered AI workflows.

**Assumed buyer:** Agency operator managing creative requests across landing pages, static ads, and short-form video

**Assumed pain:** Every new client request requires rewriting prompts for different tools and formats; workaround is maintaining separate docs and examples by channel; cost is production friction and inconsistent output quality across team members.

**Assumed pricing:** $39-99 per seat monthly because buyers compare it to other workflow utilities, but price ceiling stays low until outputs are proven.

**Distribution hypothesis:** First 10 buyers would need to come from service customers converted into beta users, because cold SaaS acquisition in this category is crowded and prompt-only value is hard to communicate.

**Strongest assumption:** Buyers want a dedicated tool for prompt generation rather than using general LLMs plus internal docs.

**Weakest assumption:** Prompt-only SaaS is already being collapsed by ChatGPT custom GPTs, Claude projects, and internal agency docs, making differentiation thin.

**Suggested Gate 3 test:** LOI - secure 5 signed beta commitments from agencies willing to pay $39+/seat after a manual concierge pilot.

**Kill signals:**
- Prospects say they can do this with ChatGPT projects or Notion AI already
- Beta users engage once then revert to existing tools
- You cannot name one proprietary workflow/input that makes outputs meaningfully better than a generic LLM

**Founder-fit notes:** Medium fit operationally, poor fit strategically right now. It requires product polish, onboarding, and differentiation in a crowded workflow layer before evidence of demand is established.


## Ranking

**Idea #2 — PROCEED FIRST**
This is closest to money already on the table. Your clients already ask for tooling help, trust you on process, and can buy a setup service without needing a polished product or design-heavy proof. It gives the fastest route to paid evidence and creates the raw material for a later product.

**Idea #1 — PROCEED IF X FAILS**
If clients refuse a service setup but still acknowledge repetitive prompt/process waste, sell a fixed-scope pack. It is easier to fulfill than SaaS and more productized than consulting, but still depends on buyers accepting your authority in a design-adjacent category.

**Idea #4 — PARK**
The software idea is too early. Distribution is weak without first proving that agencies will pay for a dedicated prompt workflow instead of using ChatGPT plus docs. Build this only after 3-5 service setups reveal a repeatable schema and one unavoidable workflow bottleneck.

**Idea #3 — KILL**
Wrong buyer and wrong price point for your current stage. Solo freelancers are budget-sensitive, hard to aggregate from your current network, and likely to consume free prompt content rather than pay recurring fees.


## What to do next

- **This week:** Pitch a paid 'AI Prompt Workflow Setup' add-on to 5 warm contacts (Gunnar, TJ, Jen, Son, Karis) with a one-page offer: audit current asset workflow, build client-specific prompt library, define reuse rules, and hand over a searchable prompt system.
- **This month:** Deliver 3 manual setups, document exactly which inputs repeat across clients (brief format, asset taxonomy, prompt modules, channel adaptations), then decide whether the repeatable artifact is a pack or early software.
- **Open question:** Do your warm buyers actually value prompt standardization enough to pay, or do they mainly want broader scoping and tooling advice where prompts are only a small piece?

## Constraints flagged

- You are not a designer, so anything sold to designers must be framed as workflow operations and reuse systems, not taste or visual direction.
- Your best distribution is existing client relationships and Slack access; avoid ideas that require broad creator audience building from scratch.
- Long unclear sales cycles are a dealbreaker, so favor add-ons and fixed-scope pilots over net-new software sales.
- Technical scoping is a weak spot, which makes prompt-only SaaS riskier until manual service data clarifies the product surface area.
