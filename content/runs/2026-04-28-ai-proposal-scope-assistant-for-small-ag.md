## Per-idea analysis

### Idea #1: Proposal Scope Copilot

**Hypothesis:** Small agencies and freelance operators will pay for a tool or service that turns raw client inquiries into a clearer recommended scope, budget framing, proposal draft, and risk notes, especially when they currently depend on a founder or senior partner to assess complexity.

**Assumed buyer:** marketing leads, operators, freelance marketers, and boutique agency owners handling inbound leads and proposal writing for small-to-mid ticket service work

**Assumed pain:** when a client sends a vague request, the operator must decode goals, estimate scope, identify unknowns, and make the budget feel workable; this is slow, mentally draining, and risky when technical complexity is unclear or a senior person must step in before a proposal can be sent

**Assumed pricing:** $49-149 per month for a lightweight tool, or $99-299 one-time / setup-style offer for a manual-plus-AI proposal pack; initial validation can start as a paid service before software

**Distribution hypothesis:** first buyers come from existing and former clients, operator peers, agency contacts, and warm Slack relationships where proposal pain already appears; demos can use before/after examples from real anonymized inquiries

**Strongest assumption:** the pain is real, frequent, and close to money because faster, better scoping directly affects close rate and team bandwidth

**Weakest assumption:** buyers may prefer this as a service layer or internal workflow rather than standalone software, so product packaging may need iteration

**Suggested Gate 3 test:** Concierge MVP - take 10 real client inquiries from agency/freelance operators and deliver a paid output including scope recommendation, clarifying questions, budget-fit options, and proposal draft; success means at least 5 pay and at least 3 use the output in a real client conversation within 14 days

**Kill signals:**
- Operators say they only trust human senior staff for scoping and would not rely on structured AI-assisted output
- Most leads are too custom or technical for a repeatable framework even with manual support
- Users like the output but do not see enough proposal volume to pay recurring pricing

**Founder-fit notes:** This is the strongest fit with Dewi's actual workflow, buyer access, and stated desire. It directly addresses dependence on Arya for complex scoping, matches logical decision-making style, and can be tested manually with existing clients and repeat contacts. It also avoids the design-credibility problem from the original idea.

### Idea #2: Proof-of-Work Finder

**Hypothesis:** Service businesses with multiple past client engagements will pay for a searchable project database that helps them instantly find relevant examples, references, and outcomes to strengthen proposals and sales conversations.

**Assumed buyer:** small agencies, freelance operators, and boutique consultancies with repeat work history but no clean internal system for retrieving proof-of-work

**Assumed pain:** past projects, testimonials, outcomes, and references are scattered across Notion, Slack, drives, invoices, and memory; when a new lead asks for relevant examples, the team wastes time assembling proof and often sends weak or generic references instead of the best matching case

**Assumed pricing:** $19-79 per month SaaS, or $200-500 one-time setup/organization service for early validation with optional subscription for search and sharing

**Distribution hypothesis:** sell first to warm client/operator network by positioning it as a sales enablement system rather than a knowledge-management tool; show how it shortens proposal turnaround and increases buyer trust

**Strongest assumption:** buyers already feel the pain in live sales workflows and will value quick retrieval of relevant proof enough to pay for organization and search

**Weakest assumption:** if current project volume is low, buyers may tolerate manual retrieval and not feel enough urgency for a dedicated product

**Suggested Gate 3 test:** Manual MVP - organize 5 operators' past work into a simple searchable database with tags for industry, project type, outcomes, and client fit; charge for setup and measure whether they use it in at least 3 sales/proposal moments each within 30 days

**Kill signals:**
- Users like the organization result but do not revisit it after setup
- The problem is seen as a one-time clean-up task rather than recurring software value
- Buyers default to manually asking teammates for examples instead of using the hub

**Founder-fit notes:** This is highly aligned with Dewi's second stated product desire and current operating context. It leverages project management and onboarding skills, can be sold to existing relationships, and does not require design capability. Main caution: may validate first as a service/workflow product before becoming software.

### Idea #3: Content Reuse Engine for Service Teams

**Hypothesis:** Lean service teams will pay for a structured system that converts existing client insights, blog posts, proposals, and social posts into repeatable multi-channel content without starting from scratch each time.

**Assumed buyer:** freelancers, boutique agencies, and operator-led teams who already create some written content but lack a reliable repurposing workflow

**Assumed pain:** they have useful raw material from client work, blogs, strategy docs, and social posts, but content creation is inconsistent because turning one source into multiple assets takes extra planning and coordination; as a result, thought leadership and demand gen stay sporadic

**Assumed pricing:** $29-99 per month for templates and workflow software, or $99-250 as a one-time setup/productized system for initial validation

**Distribution hypothesis:** start with founder/operator audience on X, LinkedIn, and warm clients by showing concrete transformation examples: one blog into threads, posts, newsletter snippets, and sales collateral

**Strongest assumption:** the buyer already believes in content and has enough raw material that repurposing saves real effort

**Weakest assumption:** this market is crowded with generic AI content tools, so differentiation must come from service-business workflow fit rather than content generation alone

**Suggested Gate 3 test:** Pilot a paid done-with-you system for 5 users where one existing source asset is converted into a monthly content pack and scheduling workflow; validate if they reuse it consistently and ask for repeat cycles

**Kill signals:**
- Users say they would rather just use ChatGPT or existing generic tools
- Repurposed outputs are appreciated but not published consistently, meaning the bottleneck is not workflow
- Differentiation is too weak versus templates and current AI writing assistants

**Founder-fit notes:** Strong fit with Dewi's content execution strengths and explicit product interest. Best improved by narrowing to service businesses and content reuse from real client work, rather than competing as a generic AI content tool.

### Idea #4: Silent Portfolio Detector

**Hypothesis:** Freelance designers will pay for a fast diagnostic that tells them exactly why their portfolio and social presence are not converting into inquiries, if the output feels sharper than generic marketing advice.

**Assumed buyer:** independent brand designer, web designer, or 2-5 person design studio owner with active Instagram, LinkedIn, Dribbble, or portfolio site but inconsistent inbound leads

**Assumed pain:** after a slow month or multiple weeks with no qualified inquiries, they review peers, keep posting, tweak captions or portfolio copy manually, and lose time while pipeline stays thin; cost is stalled revenue and random self-branding work that does not change conversion

**Assumed pricing:** $29-79 one-time audit for solo designers, with optional $149 manual teardown upsell; low enough for impulse buy, high enough to validate willingness to pay before building deeper workflow features

**Distribution hypothesis:** first 10 buyers come from direct outreach to warm operator network adjacent to creative freelancers, posting teardown-style content on X/LinkedIn, and manual audits offered in design communities, Contra/Dribbble circles, and past client referral edges

**Strongest assumption:** designers feel the pain acutely enough to pay cash for diagnosis rather than keep using free peer feedback or generic content advice

**Weakest assumption:** the founder has a credible distribution path into designer audiences; current strongest access is agency and marketing operators, not designers at scale

**Suggested Gate 3 test:** Manual Service - sell 10 paid audits at $49+ within 14 days via direct outreach and landing page; at least 4 buyers must request follow-up help or ask implementation questions

**Kill signals:**
- Less than 3 paid audits from 50+ targeted outreach messages to designers and small studios
- Buyers say the report is useful but would only use free versions, templates, or peer critique
- Most objections are credibility-based: 'why should you audit a designer portfolio if you are not a designer?'

**Founder-fit notes:** This can be improved by narrowing the frame from design critique to conversion and inquiry-generation audit, but it is still weaker than the operator-facing ideas because buyer access and trust are worse. Best kept as a secondary experiment, not the main bet.


## Ranking

**Idea #1 — PROCEED FIRST**
This is the clearest high-fit opportunity because it directly matches Dewi's stated need, existing workflow, current buyer access, and revenue path. It solves a painful bottleneck tied to real money: turning inquiries into proposals faster and with more confidence. It can be tested immediately with current clients and operator peers as a concierge service before building software.

**Idea #2 — PROCEED IF X FAILS**
This is also high-fit and very practical, especially if proposal scoping turns out to be too custom for repeatable productization. The proof hub is easier to deliver manually, easier to explain, and closely connected to current sales operations. It may be slightly less urgent than proposal scoping, which is why it ranks second.

**Idea #3 — PROCEED IF X FAILS**
This is a strong fit with Dewi's execution strengths, but the market is noisier and the differentiation risk is higher. It becomes more compelling if positioned narrowly for service businesses and connected to existing client work, not as a broad AI content tool.

**Idea #4 — PARK**
The original designer audit can be improved in positioning, but it remains weaker on founder-market fit. Buyer access is less direct, credibility is more fragile, and the product drifts toward a market where design judgment may be expected even though Dewi does not want that role.

- Complex technical scoping remains a real limitation, so early versions should focus on structured recommendations, clarifying questions, and budget-fit options rather than pretending to fully solve technical estimation
- Long unclear sales cycles are a bad fit, so target buyers with frequent, direct, low-friction proposal workflows
- Small-team bandwidth means the first offer should reduce dependency on Arya without requiring heavy ongoing custom consulting
